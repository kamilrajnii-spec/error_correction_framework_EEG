Despite their importance in modern neuro-diagnostics and Brain-Computer Interfacing (BCI), high amplitude artifacts are a major limitation to the clinical use of Electroencephalogram (EEG) signals. Advanced deep learning techniques such as Artifact Removal Transformers (ART) achieve better reconstruction accuracy, but they also tend to be more complex and consequently have a high computation time that can create a "Deployment Gap" that prevents their use in real-time, closed-loop systems. In contrast, standard analysis techniques like Discrete Wavelet Transforms (DWT), Independent Component Analysis (ICA) are fast but are not adaptive enough to distinguish true cortical oscillations from high-frequency myogenic interference, especially in channel-constricted (19-channel) montages.
This thesis suggests and tests a new two-stage hybrid solution that combines the mathematical efficiency of DWT with the non-linear feature extraction capability of a 1D-Convolutional Neural Network (1D-CNN) to fill this gap. The architecture involves Stage 1 (Wavelets) to suppress the low frequency artifacts and Stage 2 (1D-CNN) to reconstruct the signal and remove the myogenic noise. The framework has been optimized for "Edge-AI" deployment on commodity hardware (Ryzen 5 3500U), so it is clinically relevant in low resource environments.
The corpus EEGdenoiseNet was used for rigorous performance benchmarking. The performance results show an optimal hybrid model with a gain of more than 5.0 dB in Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) and a real-time processing latency of less than 55ms. This meets the <100ms goal for closed-loop neuro-monitoring systems. Moreover, the model demonstrates strong zero-shot transferability to clinical datasets not seen at training time, promising it could facilitate high fidelity neuro-diagnostics in uncontrolled clinical environments.
Key words: EEG Denoising, Deep Learning, Wavelet Transform, Real-Time Systems, Edge-AI, Neuro-diagnostics.
